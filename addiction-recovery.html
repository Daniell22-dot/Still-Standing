You Can Recover

Addiction is not a moral failing. It's a complex health condition that affects the brain and behavior. Recovery is possible, and it begins with a single step.

The ARR (12 Addiction Recovery Rules) provides a structured path forward. These rules aren't about perfection—they're about progress.

We don't hate people who struggle with addiction. We hate the addiction that has taken hold of them. Everyone deserves a chance at recovery.

— Daniel Manyasa, Founder

The 12 Addiction Recovery Rules

A step-by-step guide to rebuilding your life

1 Honest Admission

Admit that addiction has made your life unmanageable. This isn't about shame—it's about clarity. Look honestly at how addiction has affected your health, relationships, work, and spirit.

Reflection Exercise:

Write down three ways addiction has negatively impacted your life. Be specific.

2 Hope in Community

Believe that recovery is possible through support. You don't have to do this alone. Find hope in others who have walked this path before you.

Connect with Community

3 Decision to Change

Make a decision to embrace a new way of life. This is the moment you choose recovery over addiction. It's a daily, sometimes hourly, decision.

4 Personal Inventory

Take a fearless moral inventory of yourself. Examine your strengths and weaknesses without judgment. Understand what led you to addiction.

5 Honest Sharing

Admit the exact nature of your wrongs to yourself and another person. Secrets keep us sick. Sharing brings healing.

6 Readiness for Change

Become entirely ready to have your character defects removed. This is about willingness, not willpower.

7 Humble Request

Humbly ask for your shortcomings to be removed. This step connects personal effort with spiritual support.

8 List of Amends

Make a list of all persons you have harmed. Include family, friends, employers, and most importantly—yourself.

9 Making Amends

Make direct amends to such people wherever possible. Except when to do so would injure them or others.

10 Daily Inventory

Continue to take personal inventory and promptly admit when you're wrong. Recovery is maintenance, not a one-time fix.

11 Spiritual Connection

Seek through prayer and meditation to improve your conscious contact with a higher power. This is about finding meaning beyond addiction.

12 Service to Others

Carry the message of recovery to others and practice these principles in all your affairs. Healing grows when shared.

Understanding Addiction

Knowledge is power in recovery

Alcohol

Signs of dependency: Needing more to feel effects, withdrawal symptoms, failed attempts to cut down.

Withdrawal timeline: 6-12 hours after last drink, peaks at 24-72 hours.

Medical support: Detox should be medically supervised for heavy users.

Illicit Drugs

Common substances: Cocaine, heroin, methamphetamine, cannabis.

Recovery path: Medical detox, behavioral therapy, support groups, relapse prevention.

Local resources: Connect with certified treatment centers in Kenya.

Prescription Drugs

Commonly abused: Opioids (painkillers), benzodiazepines (anxiety meds), stimulants (ADHD meds).

Warning signs: "Doctor shopping," taking more than prescribed, preoccupation with medication.

Other Addictions

Behavioral addictions: Gambling, gaming, shopping, pornography.

Shared characteristics: Loss of control, continued despite consequences, preoccupation.

Treatment: Similar principles apply—admission, support, behavior change.

Take the Recovery Pledge

Making a public commitment can strengthen your resolve. This pledge is between you and your future self.

I pledge to:

  1. Take my recovery one day at a time
  2. Reach out for help when I need it
  3. Be honest with myself and my support system
  4. Practice self-compassion when I struggle
  5. Support others on their recovery journey

Urgent: Medical Detox May Be Needed

Do NOT attempt to detox from alcohol or benzodiazepines alone. Sudden withdrawal can be life-threatening. Seek medical help if you experience:

  • Severe shaking or seizures
  • Hallucinations or confusion
  • Severe nausea or vomiting
  • Rapid heart rate or chest pain
